The Evolution Question

Why did search systems evolve from simple keyword matching to complex ML pipelines?

"Because grep said 'I've found 10,000 matches!' and the user said 'But which one do I actually want?'"

Not Because:

  • We could
  • LLMs are cool
  • Everyone else is doing it

But Because:

  • Each evolution solved a specific failure mode
  • Users demanded better relevance
  • Scale broke simple solutions

Our Domain Challenges:

  • Ambiguous natural language queries
  • Semi-structured, noisy documents
  • Domain-specific vocabulary
  • Latency constraints (<2 seconds!)

The Information Theory of Search

Query Completeness: Do you have all the info you need?

Document Structure: Can you extract what you need?

Feedback Density: Do you have signals to learn from?

The Fundamental Trade-offs

Structured → Simple: Use filters & rules

Rich Feedback → ML: Learn from behavior

Sparse Everything → Hybrid: Combine approaches

"GIGO (Garbage In, Garbage Out) in search: If users search for 'that thing from yesterday', even GPT-5 won't help you. Unless you know what they did yesterday... which is creepy."

The 7 Questions You Must Answer Before Building Search

1. What's in your query?

Complete fields? Natural language? Nothing?

Flight: origin, dest, date ✓
Recruiting: "senior engineer who..." 🤔

2. What's in your documents?

Structured data? Free text? User-generated chaos?

Products: SKU, price, description
Resumes: Anything goes...

3. What's your feedback density?

Clicks per query? Conversion rate? Implicit signals?

<1%: Can't learn much
>5%: LTR becomes viable

4. What's your latency budget?

100ms? 1s? 10s? Real-time or batch?

<100ms: No LLMs
<1s: Limited reranking
>1s: Full pipeline possible

5. What's your scale?

1K docs? 1M? 1B? QPS expectations?

<100K: Brute force works
>10M: Need proper indexing
>1B: Distributed systems

6. How fresh is fresh?

Static catalog? Daily updates? Real-time?

News: Minutes matter
Products: Daily is fine
Research papers: Monthly

7. What does success mean?

Precision? Recall? Diversity? Business metric?

Legal: 100% recall required
E-commerce: Conversion rate
Social: Engagement time

The Universal Truth

Your answers to these 7 questions determine your entire architecture.

Get these wrong, and no amount of ML magic will save you.

Full-Text Search: When Structure Matches Intent

✅ Use FTS When:

  • Query terms ≈ Document terms
  • Users know exact terminology
  • You need explainable results
  • Latency is critical (<50ms)

❌ FTS Fails When:

  • Vocabulary mismatch exists
  • Context determines meaning
  • Relationships matter
  • Intent ≠ Keywords

The Pre-computed Importance Pattern


def compute_term_importance(profile):
     importance_scores = {}
    
     # Position-based weighting
     for term in profile.headline:
         importance_scores[term] = 0.9 # Headline = strong signal

     for term in profile.current_title:
         importance_scores[term] = 0.8

     for term in profile.skills:
         importance_scores[term] = 0.6

     # TF-IDF adjustment
     for term, score in importance_scores.items():
         tf = term_frequency(term, profile)
         idf = inverse_doc_frequency(term, all_profiles)
         importance_scores[term] *= tf * idf

     return importance_scores

Classic FTS Failure Mode:

Query: "Senior engineer who's built distributed systems"

Returns:

  • "HR Manager - hiring distributed systems engineers" ❌
  • "QA Engineer - tested distributed systems" ❌
  • "Junior Developer - interested in distributed systems" ❌

Problem: Token presence ≠ Semantic role

The Critical Lesson: Retrieval Depth > Ranking Quality

🎯 Fundamental Truth

If your top-2000 from FTS doesn't contain good candidates, no amount of ML/LLM reranking helps.

You can't rank what you didn't retrieve!

Invest in query expansion first
Synonym handling beats fancy ML
Recall optimization before reranking

💡 Design Decision:

If your answers to Q1 (query) and Q2 (docs) are both "structured", stop here!

Example: Flight search, inventory systems, structured databases

Vector Search: The Semantic Promise & Perils

✅ Use Embeddings When:

  • Query ≠ Document vocabulary
  • Semantic similarity matters
  • You have dense supervision
  • Cross-lingual search needed

❌ Embeddings Fail When:

  • Precision > Recall required
  • Domain has specific jargon
  • Negation matters ("NOT Java")
  • Exact matches needed

LLM vs LLM: When Acronyms Attack

Large Language Model vs Master of Laws

Generic embedding: similarity("LLM Engineer", "LLM Graduate") = 0.87 🤦

Domain matters! Law firms searching for LLMs get ML engineers.

The Critical Lesson: Retrieval Depth Still Matters

Even with perfect embeddings, if your retrieval@2000 misses candidates, reranking can't help.

Embedding search isn't magic - it's still bound by k!

💡 Design Decision:

If Q1 = "natural language" AND Q3 = "rich feedback" → Train custom embeddings

If Q3 = "sparse feedback" → Generic embeddings will disappoint

If domain-specific precision needed → Hybrid with exact match

Rule-Based Systems: When Domain Knowledge is King

✅ Use Rules When:

  • Domain has clear heuristics
  • Interpretability required
  • Sparse feedback (Q3 < 1%)
  • Business logic is complex

❌ Rules Fail When:

  • Patterns change frequently
  • Too many edge cases
  • Rich feedback available
  • Scale exceeds maintenance

The Critical Lesson: Domain Knowledge Is Your Moat

💎 Your Competitive Advantage

Generic models (embeddings, LLMs) fail on domain-specific nuances:

  • "Consultant" at Infosys ≈ "SDE" elsewhere
  • "Member of Technical Staff" ≈ "Senior Engineer"
  • "Lead" at startup ≠ "Lead" at BigCo ≠ "Lead" in India
  • "Stanford" matters more for US roles than Indian

This knowledge must be explicitly encoded somewhere!

💡 Design Decision:

If Q3 (feedback) < 1% AND you have domain expertise → Rules work well

If Q7 (success) requires interpretability → Rules are necessary

But plan for maintenance cost: O(edge cases) = O(n²) over time

Learning to Rank: When You Have Labels

✅ Use LTR When:

  • Feedback density > 5%
  • Clear success metrics
  • Features are extractable
  • Patterns are stable

❌ LTR Fails When:

  • Cold start (new domains)
  • Sparse feedback < 1%
  • Distribution shifts often
  • Features poorly represent intent

The ML Fundamental: Representation vs Labels Trade-off

Rich features + Few labels = Overfitting

Simple features + Many labels = Underfitting

Your Q3 answer (feedback density) determines feature complexity!

💡 Design Decision Tree:

Q3 > 5% dense + Q7 is quantifiable → LTR is viable

Q3 < 1% sparse → Stay with rules or use transfer learning

Q6 changes frequently → Online learning required

Remember: You need 10x more labels than features!

LLM Reranking: The Expensive Oracle

✅ Use LLMs When:

  • Nuanced judgment needed
  • Context varies per query
  • Small candidate sets (<1000)
  • Latency budget > 1s

❌ LLMs Fail When:

  • Scale requirements (millions)
  • Latency < 100ms
  • Domain knowledge missing
  • Consistency critical

The Critical Lesson: LLMs Don't Eliminate Feature Engineering - They Move It

⚠️ The Literal Interpretation Problem

Real failures we encountered with rule-like prompts:

"If candidate has 5+ years Python experience, score high" LLM: "Candidate has 4.5 years Python + 2 years Django" Score: Low ❌ (Because 4.5 < 5, ignored Django!)

LLMs follow rules too literally, missing context!

The Evolution to Rule Engines

Simple prompt → Structured prompt → Domain rules → Complex conditions

Eventually: if-else with extra steps and 1000x cost!

💡 Design Decision:

Q4 (latency) > 1s AND Q5 (scale) < 1000 → LLMs viable

Q1 (query) is very nuanced → LLMs help with edge cases

Q3 (feedback) too sparse for ML → LLMs provide pseudo-labels

Remember: LLMs are rerankers, not retrievers!

The ML Fundamental: Goodhart's Law

"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

Optimize for clicks → Clickbait results

Optimize for precision → Too conservative

Optimize for diversity → Relevance drops

💡 Design Your Metrics:

Based on Q7 (success definition):

• Legal/Medical: Recall is critical (find ALL relevant)

• E-commerce: Conversion rate matters most

• Social: Engagement + diversity balance

• B2B/Recruiting: Quality of top 10 + long-term success

Key: Use metrics to diagnose, not just evaluate!
